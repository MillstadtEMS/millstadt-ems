01Executive Summary

Millstadt Ambulance Service is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit Advanced Life Support provider that has served the residents of Southwestern St. Clair County since 1980. Operating three ambulances from a single station at 100 East Laurel Street, the Service covers approximately 107 square miles of mixed rural and suburban terrain—responding to every 911 call in its district, 24 hours a day, 365 days a year.

This document presents the organization’s financial position, operational performance, and strategic outlook for Fiscal Year 2025–2026 (May 1, 2025 through April 30, 2026). All historical financial data is drawn from audited annual reports prepared by Scheffel Boyle, CPAs, Belleville, Illinois. Current-year projections are based on the Service’s internal working budget, anchored to six months of actual operating data.

The Financial Story in Three Acts

Act 1 — Deficits (FY 2022–2023). The Service operated at a loss for at least two consecutive years. In FY2022, expenses exceeded revenue by $17,270. In FY2023, the gap narrowed to $12,912, but the trend was unsustainable. Patient fee collections could not keep pace with operating costs, and the property tax levy—while stable—was insufficient to close the difference.

Act 2 — Breakthrough (FY 2024). Patient fee revenue surged 53% to $481,243, pushing total revenue to $702,726 and producing the first surplus in multiple years: $90,037. The Service used this window to invest in critical equipment, including two Zoll X Series defibrillators ($88,895 combined).

Act 3 — New Pressure (FY 2025–2026). Call volume jumped to 879—an 18% increase in just two years. The FY 2025–2026 budget projects total revenue of $947,845 against total expenses of $1,105,568, resulting in a projected deficit of $157,723. Salaries alone are projected at $685,472, reflecting the labor market reality of staffing ambulances around the clock. Fuel, vehicle maintenance, and billing costs have all risen in lockstep with demand.

The Core Challenge

The community is calling 911 more often. The Service is answering those calls. But the cost of answering them is outpacing the revenue generated from doing so. The current property tax levy—set at approximately $0.09 per $100 of assessed valuation—represents just 35% of the authorized maximum of $0.25. That gap is the single largest untapped resource available to the organization. Even a modest increase to $0.15 would generate an additional $166,000 per year, enough to offset the projected deficit entirely.

This document lays out the facts: the demand, the costs, the revenue structure, the capital needs, and the options. The numbers come from audits and operating records, not projections built on assumptions. The intent is straightforward—to give the Board, stakeholders, and the public a clear picture of where Millstadt Ambulance Service stands and what it will take to keep it standing.

03Mission, Vision & Core Values

Mission

At Millstadt EMS, our mission is to provide exceptional, compassionate, and timely emergency medical care to our diverse community. We are dedicated to advancing health and safety through highly trained professionals, cutting-edge technology, and a commitment to continuous improvement, ensuring the well-being of every person we serve.

Vision

Our vision is to be a leader in pre-hospital care, setting the standard for emergency medical services through innovation, education, and collaboration. We strive to enhance the quality of life in our region by delivering the highest level of care, fostering community partnerships, and preparing for the challenges of tomorrow with excellence and integrity.

Core Values

Excellence
We hold ourselves to the highest clinical and operational standards—not because it is required, but because the people we serve deserve nothing less. Excellence is measured at the patient’s side, in every protocol followed and every second that counts.
Compassion
Every call begins with someone in crisis. We respond not just with training and equipment, but with genuine concern for the person in front of us. Compassion is the foundation of patient care—it cannot be taught in a textbook, but it must be present on every call.
Integrity
We do what is right even when no one is watching. Our financial records are audited. Our clinical decisions are documented. Our commitments to the community are kept. Trust is earned through consistent, honest action—not through words alone.
Accountability
Every dollar spent, every decision made, and every patient outcome is owned. When we fall short, we identify the cause and fix it. Accountability is not about assigning blame—it is about taking responsibility for results and being transparent about them.
Collaboration
No ambulance service operates alone. We work alongside fire departments, law enforcement, hospitals, mutual aid agencies, and the community itself. Collaboration multiplies what a small service can accomplish and ensures that no gap in coverage goes unaddressed.
Preparedness
Emergencies do not wait for convenient timing. Our ambulances are stocked, our crews are trained, and our equipment is maintained before the call comes in. Preparedness is the discipline of being ready every hour of every day—because the next call could be the one that matters most.
04Pillars of Excellence
Millstadt EMS at a community event — the Service maintains a visible presence beyond emergency response, reinforcing its role as a community partner.

Five operational pillars define how Millstadt Ambulance Service delivers on its mission. Each pillar represents a non-negotiable commitment—not a future goal, but a current standard that the organization maintains every day it puts ambulances on the road.

I
Clinical Excellence
The Service operates at the Advanced Life Support level with Zoll X Series cardiac monitors, Stryker Power Load patient handling systems, and ESO electronic documentation. Providers carry a full complement of ALS medications—from cardiac interventions to advanced airway management—capabilities that directly affect patient outcomes in the critical minutes before hospital arrival.
II
Operational Readiness
Three ambulances cover 107 square miles, 24 hours a day, with no scheduled downtime. Mutual aid agreements with seven neighboring EMS agencies provide a safety net during simultaneous calls, but the goal is to handle every incident within the district using district resources. Readiness is not a policy—it is the product of staffing, maintenance, and supply chain discipline.
III
Financial Stewardship
Every dollar of public funding is tracked, audited, and reported. Annual financial reports are prepared by an independent CPA firm. The Board reviews monthly expenditures line by line. When the Service ran deficits in FY2022 and FY2023, it did not defer the problem—it identified the revenue shortfall, improved billing processes, and produced a surplus in FY2024. Transparency is the standard, not the exception.
IV
Community Partnership
Millstadt Ambulance Service exists because the community created it in 1980. It answers to the residents it serves through a publicly appointed Board, open financial reporting, and a commitment to respond to every call regardless of the patient’s ability to pay. The relationship is not transactional—it is foundational.
V
Workforce Investment
Recruiting and retaining qualified paramedics and EMTs is the single most important operational challenge in EMS. The Service competes for talent in a regional labor market that includes larger, better-funded agencies. Competitive wages, structured scheduling through W2W, and investments in training and equipment are not perks—they are the baseline requirements for keeping ambulances staffed and crews capable.
05District Overview & History
The 2025 Millstadt Ambulance Service Open House — Millstadt EMS units alongside response partners from the community.

Millstadt Ambulance Service has served the residents of Southwestern St. Clair County since 1980. Originally established to address the emergency medical needs of a growing rural and suburban community, the Service has evolved over more than four decades into a capable ALS-level provider operating around the clock, 365 days a year.

The Service is organized as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization and was annexed into the Millstadt Fire Protection District. While it operates independently of the Fire District in its day-to-day functions, it receives a portion of its funding through a property tax levy administered by the District. The Service is exempt from federal and state income taxes under Section 501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code.

Headquartered at 100 East Laurel Street in the Village of Millstadt, the Service provides emergency ambulance coverage to the Village of Millstadt, Millstadt Township, and the western portion of Stookey Township. It also provides and receives mutual aid with seven neighboring EMS agencies—Monroe County EMS, Columbia EMS, Abbott EMS, Medstar EMS, Dupo EMS, Mascoutah EMS, and O’Fallon EMS—extending the safety net across a wide section of the Metro East region.

What the Trend Means

This trajectory has significant implications for every aspect of the operation:

  • Staffing pressure: Each call requires a minimum two-person crew. As volume climbs, the probability of simultaneous calls rises—stretching a lean roster and increasing reliance on mutual aid.
  • Fleet utilization: More calls mean more miles, more engine hours, and faster wear on vehicles already past their recommended service life.
  • Supply consumption: Every patient contact consumes medical supplies—from IV catheters to cardiac medications. A 135-call increase is not marginal; it is a measurable draw on the supply chain.
  • Operational strain: At 879 calls per year across three ambulances, each unit averages nearly 293 responses annually—approaching the practical ceiling for a service of this size without additional capacity.

Each additional call represents fuel consumed, medical supplies used, wear on vehicles, and hours worked by personnel who are already stretched thin. When call volume rises by nearly a fifth in two years, the strain is not theoretical—it is felt on every shift.

09Operational Impact of Growth
Unit 3926 on scene of a motor vehicle accident — each of the 879 calls in 2025 represents a real incident requiring personnel, equipment, and supplies.

An 18% increase in call volume over two years does not arrive in isolation. It cascades through every operational layer of a small ambulance service, compounding costs and compressing the margin for error.

Staffing Pressure

With only three ambulances and a lean crew roster, Millstadt EMS is absorbing 135 additional calls per year compared to 2023. Every call requires a minimum two-person crew. As volume climbs, so does the probability of simultaneous calls—situations where the district must rely on mutual aid because no local unit is available. Crew fatigue accumulates in ways that are not immediately visible on a balance sheet but carry real consequences for patient care and provider safety.

Fleet Wear & Maintenance

More calls mean more miles. The Service’s motor fuel expenses have risen from $5,446 in FY2022 to $9,228 in FY2024—a 69% increase. Repair and maintenance costs fluctuate with the age and condition of the fleet, but the trend line is clear: vehicles that are running more often require more frequent service. In February 2026, a single transmission and turbo repair on one unit cost $13,712—underscoring the unpredictable nature of maintaining an aging fleet under heavy use.

Medical Supply Consumption

Medical supply and paramedic assistance costs more than doubled over two years, rising from $6,714 in FY2022 to $14,797 in FY2024. This is a direct function of call volume: more patients treated means more supplies consumed. Every patient contact draws from the Service’s inventory of ALS medications and medical supplies—IV catheters, cardiac drugs, airway equipment—all of which must be restocked after use.

Response Coverage

With three ambulances covering 107 square miles, the probability of a coverage gap during a multi-call period grows with every percentage point of volume increase. When one or two units are committed to active calls, the remaining unit may be positioned at the far end of the district. Response times in those moments extend, and the Service must depend on mutual aid partners who face their own workload pressures.

The Compounding Effect

Each additional call does not carry a fixed marginal cost. At low volumes, the existing infrastructure absorbs incremental demand. But as volume approaches the system’s practical capacity, the cost curve steepens—overtime increases, equipment breaks down faster, supplies need emergency restocking, and the risk of a call going unanswered rises. Millstadt EMS is operating in the steep part of that curve.

Patient Fees — The Growth Engine

Patient fee revenue increased from $276,649 in FY2022 to $481,243 in FY2024—a gain of $204,594, or 74%, over two years. This growth was driven by two factors: rising call volume (more patients transported) and improved billing and collection processes. By FY2024, patient fees represented 68% of total revenue, up from 56% in FY2022.

Patient billing in EMS is inherently challenging. The Service provides care regardless of a patient’s ability to pay, insurance status, or coverage level. Medicare and Medicaid reimburse at fixed rates that often fall below the cost of providing the service. The allowance for uncollectible accounts stood at $33,127 (approximately 10% of gross receivables) as of April 30, 2024. The Board discontinues collection attempts on accounts with no activity for six months.

13Financial Narrative: Deficit to Surplus to New Challenges

The Deficit Years (FY 2022 – FY 2023)

For at least two consecutive fiscal years, Millstadt Ambulance Service spent more than it earned. In FY2022, expenses exceeded revenue by $17,270. In FY2023, the gap narrowed to $12,912, but the Service was still drawing down its net assets to cover operations. The core problem was structural: patient fee collections were not keeping pace with the cost of running the ambulances, and property tax revenue—while stable—was not sufficient to close the gap.

During these years, the Service did not have the financial room to invest meaningfully in equipment replacement, training, or staffing improvements. Capital decisions were reactive rather than strategic. The organization was, in effect, deferring costs that would inevitably come due.

The Breakthrough (FY 2024)

FY2024 marked a turning point. Total revenue jumped to $702,726—an increase of $168,759, or 31.6%, over the prior year. The driver was patient fee collections, which surged from $314,666 to $481,243. This $166,577 increase (53%) reflected both higher call volume (more patients transported and billed) and what appears to be improved billing and collection activity.

Expenses grew more modestly, rising $65,810 (12%) to $612,689. The result was a surplus of $90,037—the first positive net position in at least three years. The Service used this window to make critical capital investments, including two Zoll X Series defibrillators ($88,895 combined) and to begin addressing deferred equipment needs.

The Current Reality (FY 2025–2026)

The surplus of FY2024 provided breathing room, but it did not resolve the underlying financial challenges facing the Service. The FY 2025–2026 budget projects total revenue of $947,845 against total expenses of $1,105,568—a projected deficit of approximately $157,723.

Several factors are driving this renewed pressure:

  • Salary growth: Projected at $685,472 for FY25-26—nearly double FY2022 levels—reflecting the labor market reality of recruiting and retaining qualified EMS personnel.
  • Billing and insurance costs: At $70,382 projected, billing service fees have more than doubled as revenue (and the complexity of collections) has grown.
  • Fuel and vehicle costs: Motor fuel ($47,410) and ambulance repairs ($42,179) reflect both higher utilization and an aging fleet.
  • New debt service: Truck payments ($36,000) and mortgage ($25,696) consume capital that might otherwise support operations.
The Central Tension

Call volume is rising. The community is calling 911 more often. The Service is answering those calls. But the cost of answering them is outpacing the revenue generated from doing so. The FY2024 surplus was real, but it was not a permanent fix. Without a structural adjustment to the revenue base—whether through the property tax levy, grant funding, or other mechanisms—the Service will return to deficit operations.

16Tax Levy & Revenue Capacity

Millstadt Ambulance Service receives its property tax revenue through the Millstadt Fire Protection District, which levies a rate on the Equalized Assessed Valuation (EAV) of real property within the district. The maximum authorized tax rate for ambulance service is $0.2500 per $100 of assessed valuation.

The district is currently levying at approximately $0.09 per $100 EAV—roughly 35% of its authorized maximum. This is not a cap imposed by external constraints; it is a rate the district has historically chosen to levy.

Grant revenue has been minimal in recent years. The Service received $7,318 in grant funding in FY2022, but no grant revenue was recorded in FY2023 or FY2024. Given the financial pressures the organization faces, pursuing federal and state grant opportunities represents a meaningful area for growth.

18Future Outlook

The trajectory facing Millstadt Ambulance Service is clear. Call volume is growing. Operating costs are rising. The current revenue structure—heavily dependent on patient fee collections with a property tax levy set well below its authorized ceiling—is not keeping pace.

What the Data Tells Us

  • Call volume grew 18.15% in two years and shows no sign of leveling off. Population trends, an aging demographic, and increased healthcare utilization in the region all point to continued demand growth.
  • Revenue has been volatile: FY2024 saw a 32% revenue jump driven by patient billing, but FY22 and FY23 both ran deficits. Patient fee revenue is inherently unpredictable and should not be the primary funding source for essential public safety infrastructure.
  • The fleet is aging: The 2015 ambulance has crossed the 10-year threshold. Replacement costs will be substantial, and deferring that decision only increases the risk of an unplanned service interruption.
  • Staffing costs will continue to rise: The EMS labor market is competitive. Retaining qualified paramedics and EMTs requires compensation that reflects the skill, risk, and demands of the profession.
  • Tax capacity exists: The district is using only 35% of its authorized levy. There is room to increase revenue without requiring voter approval for a rate above the existing cap.

Strategic Priorities

Reserve Position

As of May 1, 2025, the Service held approximately $80,000 in its money market account. The ending money market balance as of October 31, 2025 was approximately $52,000.

These reserve movements illustrate the cash flow challenges the Service faces even during normal operations. Without adequate operating revenue, reserves intended for capital needs and contingencies are instead used to smooth cash flow gaps—a practice that, if sustained, will erode the financial cushion entirely.

20Commitment to the Community

Serving Millstadt Since 1980

Forty-five years ago, the residents of this district decided they needed their own ambulance service. That decision has been validated every year since—most recently, 879 times in 2025 alone.

This document was prepared to provide the Board of Directors, elected officials, and the public with a transparent and complete picture of the financial and operational position of Millstadt Ambulance Service. Every financial figure is traceable to an audited report or a recorded transaction. Nothing has been estimated for convenience, and no projections have been made without supporting data.

The facts are straightforward. The community’s demand for emergency medical services is growing. The Service is meeting that demand with three ambulances, a dedicated crew, and clinical capabilities that exceed what most agencies of this size can offer. But the revenue structure has not kept pace with the cost of delivering that service. The property tax levy—the one revenue source the district directly controls—remains at 35% of its authorized capacity.

The purpose of this document is not to advocate for a specific course of action. It is to lay the groundwork for an informed decision. Whether the path forward involves a levy adjustment, grant funding, operational restructuring, or some combination of all three, the Board and the community now have the numbers to evaluate the options clearly.

Millstadt Ambulance Service does not operate for profit. It does not have shareholders. It does not have a marketing budget. It has ambulances, medics, and a responsibility to answer when someone calls 911. The question this budget addresses is not whether that work should continue—it is how it will be funded.

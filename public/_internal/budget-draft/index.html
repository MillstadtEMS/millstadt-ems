01Executive Summary

Millstadt Ambulance Service is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit Advanced Life Support provider serving approximately 107 square miles of Southwestern St. Clair County from a single station at 100 East Laurel Street. Three ambulances, two EMS Chiefs, and a roster of paramedics and EMTs answer every 911 call in the district around the clock.

This document presents the Service's financial position, operational performance, and strategic outlook for Fiscal Year 2025–2026. Historical data draws from audited financial reports prepared by Scheffel Boyle, CPAs, Belleville, Illinois. Current-year figures come from the Millstadt Ambulance Financial Master workbook, which tracks every vendor payment and expense transaction by month and account.

Three Fiscal Years, Three Realities

FY 2023 — Deficit. Revenue of $533,967 fell short of $546,879 in expenses, producing a loss of $12,912. Patient collections lagged behind operating costs. The property tax levy, while stable, could not close the gap.

FY 2024 — Surplus. Patient fee revenue surged 53% to $481,243, driven by higher call volume and improved billing. Total revenue reached $702,726 against $612,689 in expenses, yielding a surplus of $90,037. The Service invested in two Zoll X Series cardiac monitor/defibrillators ($88,895) during this window.

FY 2025–2026 — Renewed Pressure. Through eight months of the current fiscal year, the Service has recorded $651,553 in operating expenses — already exceeding the full prior-year total of $583,854. Vehicle costs have tripled. Medical equipment spending is up 51%. Payroll and benefits continue to climb. Call volume hit 879 in calendar year 2025, an 18% increase in two years, and every additional call compounds the cost structure.

The Central Question

The district's property tax levy sits at roughly $0.09 per $100 of assessed valuation — 35% of its authorized ceiling of $0.25. That unused capacity represents the single largest revenue tool available. A modest adjustment to $0.15 would generate an additional $166,000 annually. Whether and how to use that tool is the decision this document is built to inform.

03Mission, Vision & Core Values

Mission

At Millstadt EMS, our mission is to provide exceptional, compassionate, and timely emergency medical care to our diverse community. We are dedicated to advancing health and safety through highly trained professionals, cutting-edge technology, and a commitment to continuous improvement, ensuring the well-being of every person we serve.

Vision

Our vision is to be a leader in pre-hospital care, setting the standard for emergency medical services through innovation, education, and collaboration. We strive to enhance the quality of life in our region by delivering the highest level of care, fostering community partnerships, and preparing for the challenges of tomorrow with excellence and integrity.

Core Values

Excellence
We hold ourselves to the highest clinical and operational standards — not because regulation demands it, but because the person on the stretcher deserves nothing less.
Compassion
Every call starts with someone in distress. We respond with skill, but we lead with empathy. Technical competence without human concern is not emergency medicine.
Integrity
Our finances are audited. Our clinical decisions are documented. Our commitments are kept. The public does not need to take our word for it — the records are open.
Accountability
Every dollar, every decision, every patient outcome is owned. When something falls short, we find out why and fix it. That is what accountability looks like in practice.
Collaboration
We operate within a regional EMS system — alongside hospitals, fire departments, law enforcement, and seven mutual aid agencies. No single service succeeds alone.
Preparedness
Emergencies do not schedule themselves. Ambulances are stocked, crews are trained, and equipment is maintained before the tones drop — because the next call could be the worst one.
04Pillars of Excellence

Six pillars define how Millstadt Ambulance Service operates. Each reflects a commitment that the organization maintains on every shift, not an aspiration it hopes to reach someday. These pillars are defensible in a public meeting because they describe what the Service actually does.

I
Clinical Excellence
The Service operates at the ALS level with Zoll X Series cardiac monitors, Stryker Power Load patient handling, and a full medication inventory covering cardiac, respiratory, pain management, and critical care interventions. Clinical protocols are governed by the Southwestern Illinois EMS System Medical Director. The standard is not "adequate" — it is the same level of field care available in larger metro systems.
II
Operational Readiness
Three ambulances staff 107 square miles, 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. There is no scheduled downtime, no seasonal reduction, and no hours where a call goes unanswered. When a unit is committed, the remaining fleet covers the district. Readiness is not a policy document — it is the daily product of scheduling, maintenance, supply chain discipline, and crew availability.
III
Workforce Sustainability
Recruiting and retaining paramedics and EMTs is the defining operational challenge in EMS nationwide. Millstadt competes for the same labor pool as larger, better-funded agencies in the Metro East. Wages must be competitive. Scheduling must be sustainable. Training must be ongoing. The alternative — chronic understaffing — is not a theoretical risk; it is what happens when workforce investment falls behind.
IV
Community Trust & Accountability
The Service is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit funded by public tax dollars and patient fees. Its financial records are audited annually by an independent CPA firm. Board meetings are open. This budget document exists because the public has a right to see exactly where the money goes and why. Transparency is not optional for an organization that depends on community support to operate.
V
System Capacity & Response Reliability
At 879 calls per year across three units, the Service averages 293 responses per ambulance. As volume rises, so does the probability of simultaneous calls — events where every available unit is committed and the district must depend on mutual aid. Capacity planning is not about expansion for its own sake; it is about ensuring that the next 911 caller gets an ambulance from their own district, not a neighboring agency 20 minutes away.
VI
Regional Integration
Millstadt operates within a network of seven mutual aid partners: Monroe County EMS, Columbia EMS, Abbott EMS, Medstar EMS, Dupo EMS, Mascoutah EMS, and O'Fallon EMS. The Service also operates under the clinical governance of the Southwestern Illinois EMS System. Regional coordination is not a courtesy — it is a structural requirement for covering 107 square miles with three ambulances.
05District Overview & History
The 2025 Millstadt Ambulance Service Open House — Millstadt EMS units alongside response partners from the community.

Millstadt Ambulance Service has operated continuously since 1980. It is organized as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit annexed into the Millstadt Fire Protection District, receiving a portion of its funding through a property tax levy. The Service is exempt from federal and state income taxes.

The district covers the Village of Millstadt, Millstadt Township, and the western portion of Stookey Township — approximately 107 square miles of rural, suburban, agricultural, and light industrial terrain in St. Clair County, Illinois.

Operational Consequences

  • Staffing: Each call requires a two-person crew minimum. At 879 calls, crews are running significantly more responses per shift than two years ago. Fatigue compounds over time and directly affects provider safety and patient care quality.
  • Fleet wear: More responses mean more miles, more engine hours, and shorter intervals between required maintenance. The 2015 ambulance is past its recommended service life and absorbing heavier use than ever.
  • Supply consumption: Every patient contact depletes the medication and supply inventory. A 135-call increase is not marginal — it is a measurable draw that tracks directly to the medical supply budget line.
  • Simultaneous call exposure: At 879 calls across three units, the statistical probability of overlapping calls — where every ambulance is committed — has increased meaningfully. When that happens, the district depends on mutual aid.
09Operational Impact of Growth
Unit 3926 on scene — each of the 879 calls in 2025 requires personnel, equipment, fuel, and medical supplies.

The master spreadsheet confirms what the call volume data predicts: costs are rising across every operational category.

Vehicle Costs — Up 299%

Vehicle expenses jumped from $13,023 in FY 2024-25 to $51,920 through eight months of FY 2025-26. The largest drivers: a new truck loan ($3,000/month starting June 2025, totaling $15,000 so far) and vehicle maintenance that spiked from $4,186 to $23,690. One transmission and turbo repair alone cost $13,712 in February 2026. Aging vehicles under heavier use break down more often and cost more to fix.

Medical Operations — Up 57%

Medical equipment and supply spending rose from $44,341 to $69,469. The bulk is equipment payments to Stryker and Zoll — the Power Load system and cardiac monitors that are financed over time. Medical gas (Airgas) costs rose 18% on volume alone.

Personnel — Up 6%

Wages, payroll taxes, 401(k) contributions, and payroll processing grew from $373,084 to $395,954. This is the single largest expense category at 61% of total spending. The increase reflects both staffing needs and labor market pressure — the same market conditions every EMS agency in the region is navigating.

Eight Months, Already Over Last Year

FY 2025-26 expenses through March 2026 total $651,553 — exceeding the entire FY 2024-25 by $67,699, with one month still remaining. The run rate is not sustainable at the current revenue level.

13Financial Narrative

The Deficit Period (FY 2022–2023)

The Service operated at a loss for at least two consecutive years. FY2022 produced a $17,270 shortfall; FY2023 narrowed the gap to $12,912 but remained negative. Patient fee collections were not growing fast enough to cover a cost structure driven by 24/7 staffing, vehicle maintenance, and medical supply requirements.

The Surplus Window (FY 2024)

Patient fees surged from $314,666 to $481,243 — a 53% jump — while expenses grew a more controlled 12%. The $90,037 surplus allowed the Service to invest in two Zoll X Series defibrillators and begin addressing deferred capital needs.

The Current Fiscal Year (FY 2025-26)

Eight months into the fiscal year, expenses total $651,553. That is already $67,699 more than the full 12 months of FY 2024-25. The drivers are not discretionary spending. Vehicle maintenance hit $23,690 after a series of mechanical failures on aging units. A new truck loan added $3,000/month. Medical equipment financing for the Stryker and Zoll systems totals $57,968 year-to-date. Personnel costs — the largest category at 61% of total expenses — rose 6.1% on payroll and benefits alone.

Revenue data for FY 2025-26 is not yet available in audited form. Based on the FY 2024 revenue trajectory ($702,726), the current expense pace would require comparable or higher revenue to avoid a deficit. Whether that materializes depends on patient fee collections, payer mix, and the timing of tax distributions.

The Structural Issue

The FY 2024 surplus was driven primarily by a one-time surge in patient fee collections. Patient fees are inherently unpredictable — dependent on call mix, payer type, insurance reimbursement rates, and collection success. Building a sustainable operation on the most volatile revenue source is a structural risk. The property tax levy — the most stable and controllable revenue stream — remains at 35% of its authorized capacity.

18Future Outlook

Strategic Priorities

1. Revenue Stabilization: The gap between the current levy rate and the authorized maximum is the most direct path to sustainable funding. Continued reliance on patient fee volatility is a structural risk.
2. Fleet Replacement: The 2015 ambulance is past its recommended service life. A replacement plan that avoids emergency purchasing and spreads cost over multiple years is overdue.
3. Capacity Planning: At 879 calls and rising, the three-unit model is approaching its practical limit. The fourth ambulance analysis ($378,432/year in staffing alone) quantifies what expansion would require.
4. Grant Pursuit: Two consecutive years of zero grant revenue represents a missed opportunity. Federal and state programs exist specifically for rural and suburban EMS agencies facing the pressures this district is experiencing.

20Commitment to the Community

Forty-five years ago, the residents of this district decided they needed their own ambulance service. That decision has been validated every year since — most recently, 879 times in 2025.

Every figure in this document traces to an audited financial report or a recorded transaction in the operating ledger. Nothing has been estimated for convenience. Where data is not yet available — such as FY 2025-26 revenue — that limitation is stated directly rather than filled with assumptions.

The facts are straightforward. Call volume is growing. Costs are rising across every category. The fleet is aging under heavier use. The revenue base — stable on the tax side, volatile on the billing side — has not kept pace. The property tax levy remains at 35% of its authorized capacity.

This is not an organization in crisis. It is an organization facing a decision. The demand is real. The costs are documented. The revenue tools exist. What remains is the will to use them.

01Executive Summary

Millstadt Ambulance Service is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit Advanced Life Support provider serving approximately 107 square miles of Southwestern St. Clair County from a single station at 100 East Laurel Street. Three ambulances, two EMS Chiefs, and a roster of paramedics and EMTs answer every 911 call in the district around the clock.

This document presents the Service's financial position, operational performance, and strategic outlook for Fiscal Year 2025–2026. Historical data draws from audited financial reports prepared by Scheffel Boyle, CPAs, Belleville, Illinois. Current-year figures come from the Millstadt Ambulance Financial Master workbook, which tracks every vendor payment and expense transaction by month and account.

Three Fiscal Years, Three Realities

FY 2023 — Deficit. Revenue of $533,967 fell short of $546,879 in expenses, producing a loss of $12,912.

FY 2024 — Surplus. Patient fee revenue surged 53% to $481,243. Total revenue reached $702,726 against $612,689 in expenses, yielding a surplus of $90,037. The Service invested in two Zoll X Series cardiac monitor/defibrillators ($88,895).

FY 2025–2026 — Renewed Pressure. Through eight months, the Service has recorded $651,553 in operating expenses — already exceeding the full prior-year total of $583,854. Vehicle costs have tripled. Medical equipment spending is up 51%. Call volume hit 879 in 2025, an 18% increase in two years.

The Central Question

The district's property tax levy sits at roughly $0.09 per $100 of assessed valuation — 35% of its authorized ceiling of $0.25. That unused capacity represents the single largest revenue tool available. A modest adjustment to $0.15 would generate an additional $166,000 annually. Whether and how to use that tool is the decision this document is built to inform.

03Mission, Vision & Core Values
Millstadt EMS crew with community members at the station — the values that guide every interaction.

Mission

At Millstadt EMS, our mission is to provide exceptional, compassionate, and timely emergency medical care to our diverse community. We are dedicated to advancing health and safety through highly trained professionals, cutting-edge technology, and a commitment to continuous improvement, ensuring the well-being of every person we serve.

Vision

Our vision is to be a leader in pre-hospital care, setting the standard for emergency medical services through innovation, education, and collaboration. We strive to enhance the quality of life in our region by delivering the highest level of care, fostering community partnerships, and preparing for the challenges of tomorrow with excellence and integrity.

Core Values

Excellence
We hold ourselves to the highest clinical and operational standards — not because regulation demands it, but because the person on the stretcher deserves nothing less.
Compassion
Every call starts with someone in distress. We respond with skill, but we lead with empathy. Technical competence without human concern is not emergency medicine.
Integrity
Our finances are audited. Our clinical decisions are documented. Our commitments are kept. The public does not need to take our word for it — the records are open.
Accountability
Every dollar, every decision, every patient outcome is owned. When something falls short, we find out why and fix it.
Collaboration
We operate within a regional EMS system — alongside hospitals, fire departments, law enforcement, and seven mutual aid agencies.
Preparedness
Emergencies do not schedule themselves. Ambulances are stocked, crews are trained, and equipment is maintained before the tones drop.
04Pillars of Excellence
Chief Kenneth James and EMT Dylan Spencer in front of the Millstadt Paramedic unit.

Six pillars define how Millstadt Ambulance Service operates. Each reflects a commitment maintained on every shift.

IClinical Excellence

ALS-level care with Zoll X Series cardiac monitors, Hamilton ventilators, Sapphire infusion pumps, point-of-care ultrasound, and Stryker Power Load patient handling. Clinical protocols governed by the SWIL EMS System Medical Director.

IIOperational Readiness

Three ambulances staff 107 square miles, 24/7/365. No scheduled downtime, no seasonal reduction, no hours where a call goes unanswered.

IIIWorkforce Sustainability

Competing for the same labor pool as larger Metro East agencies. Wages must be competitive, scheduling sustainable, and training ongoing.

IVCommunity Trust & Accountability

A 501(c)(3) nonprofit funded by public tax dollars and patient fees. Finances audited annually by an independent CPA firm. Board meetings open.

VSystem Capacity & Response Reliability

At 879 calls across three units, the Service averages 293 responses per ambulance. Capacity planning ensures the next 911 caller gets a local ambulance.

VIRegional Integration

Seven mutual aid partners: Monroe County, Columbia, Abbott, Medstar, Dupo, Mascoutah, and O'Fallon EMS. Clinical governance through the SWIL EMS System.

05District Overview & History
2025 Millstadt Ambulance Service Open House — ambulances and response partners at the station.

Millstadt Ambulance Service has operated continuously since 1980 as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit annexed into the Millstadt Fire Protection District. The district covers the Village of Millstadt, Millstadt Township, and the western portion of Stookey Township — approximately 107 square miles in St. Clair County, Illinois.

Operational Consequences

  • Staffing: Two-person crew minimum per call. Fatigue compounds over time and affects safety and care quality.
  • Fleet: More miles, more engine hours, shorter intervals between maintenance. The 2015 ambulance is past recommended service life.
  • Supplies: Every patient contact depletes inventory. A 135-call increase is a measurable draw on the supply budget.
  • Simultaneous calls: At 879 calls across three units, overlapping events force reliance on mutual aid.

13Financial Narrative

The Deficit Period (FY 2022–2023)

The Service operated at a loss for at least two consecutive years. FY2022 produced a $17,270 shortfall; FY2023 narrowed the gap to $12,912 but remained negative.

The Surplus Window (FY 2024)

Patient fees surged 53% to $481,243. Total revenue reached $702,726 against $612,689 in expenses. Surplus of $90,037 funded two Zoll X Series defibrillators ($88,895).

The Current Fiscal Year (FY 2025-26)

Eight months in, expenses total $651,553 — $67,699 more than the full prior year. Vehicle maintenance hit $23,690. New truck loan adds $3,000/month. Medical equipment financing: $57,968 year-to-date. Personnel — 61% of total expenses — rose 6.1%.

The Structural Issue

The FY 2024 surplus was driven primarily by a one-time surge in patient fee collections. Patient fees are inherently unpredictable. Building a sustainable operation on the most volatile revenue source is a structural risk. The property tax levy — the most stable and controllable stream — remains at 35% of capacity.

Unit 3926 staged and ready — the financial trajectory determines whether this level of readiness can be maintained.

20Commitment to the Community

Forty-five years ago, the residents of this district decided they needed their own ambulance service. That decision has been validated every year since — most recently, 879 times in 2025.

Every figure in this document traces to an audited financial report or a recorded transaction. Nothing has been estimated for convenience. Where data is not yet available, that limitation is stated directly.

Call volume is growing. Costs are rising across every category. The fleet is aging under heavier use. The revenue base has not kept pace. The property tax levy remains at 35% of its authorized capacity.

This is not an organization in crisis. It is an organization facing a decision. The demand is real. The costs are documented. The revenue tools exist. What remains is the will to use them.

Where it started — an early Millstadt Ambulance Service unit. Forty-five years later, the mission remains the same.
